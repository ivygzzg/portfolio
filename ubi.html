Overview

A car insurance app stuck in 2018

UBI (Usage-Based Insurance) is a mobile app that tracks driving behavior and automatically adjusts car insurance premiums based on how you actually drive — not just your age and zip code. The concept is powerful, but the existing app was failing to communicate its value.

Users were confused about how the tracking worked, what data was being collected, and why their premium changed month to month. Trust was low, onboarding completion was under 40%, and the app had a 2.3 star rating on the App Store.

Problem

Opacity kills trust in insurance

Through user interviews and usability tests with 18 participants, three core problems emerged consistently:

01

No transparency on data collection

Users didn't know what the app was tracking — GPS, speed, phone usage — or when. This created anxiety and a sense of surveillance rather than benefit.

02

Incomprehensible scoring system

The driving score was displayed as a single number with no context. Users couldn't understand what behaviors influenced it or how to improve.

03

Onboarding asked too much too fast

The app requested location permissions, vehicle details, and payment info in the first 3 screens — before explaining any value. Drop-off was immediate.

Process

Research → Define → Design → Test

I led the end-to-end design process across four phases over 12 weeks, working closely with the product manager, two engineers, and the data science team responsible for the scoring algorithm.

Design process overview — from discovery to handoff

The discovery phase included 18 user interviews, a competitive analysis of 8 insurance apps (Metromile, Root, Allstate Drivewise), and a heuristic evaluation of the existing app. Key insight: every competing app treated transparency as a feature, not a given.

I synthesized findings into a Jobs to be Done framework — users weren't just looking to save money on insurance, they wanted to feel like the app was working for them, not monitoring them.

Solution

Transparency as the core design principle

Every design decision was filtered through one question: does this help users understand what's happening and why? This shaped three major design changes.

Redesigned onboarding — value first, permissions later

The new onboarding shows users exactly how much they could save before asking for anything. Permissions are requested contextually — location access is asked only when the first trip is about to start, with a clear explanation of why it's needed and what it enables.

Before vs. After — onboarding completion improved from 38% to 74%

Driving score breakdown — from black box to glass box

The score is now broken into four visible factors: speed, braking, phone usage, and time of day. Each factor shows your trend over the last 30 days and offers one actionable tip. Users can finally see exactly what moved their score.

Trip summary — a receipt for every drive

Every trip now gets a summary screen that shows the route, score, and key moments (hard braking events, phone usage). This turned a source of anxiety into a moment of engagement — users started checking it voluntarily.

Results

Measurable impact after launch

The redesigned app launched to a beta group of 2,000 users before full rollout. Results after 8 weeks of beta:

"Now I actually understand why my insurance is the price it is. I feel like the app is finally on my side." — Beta tester feedback

The trip summary feature became unexpectedly popular — 60% of active users checked their trip summary after every drive, a behavior that wasn't anticipated and that significantly increased session frequency.

Learnings

What I'd do differently

In hindsight, I would have involved the data science team earlier in the process. The scoring algorithm had constraints I only discovered during handoff, which required some late-stage design adjustments. Closer collaboration from week one would have saved time and produced a more technically feasible first proposal.

I also underestimated how much users valued the privacy control settings — it was one of the most-praised features in feedback, but it was designed as a secondary screen. In the next iteration, I'd give it more prominence in the main navigation.
